7
â€¢ A resistive potentiometer is a device in which a displacement is changed
into resistance.
â€¢ This displacement can be either translational such as in the first figure or
rotational, such as in the second one. In fact, the second type of
potentiometer should look familiar to you. In it, the turning mechanism is
translated into a resistance value. We can also have rotational
potentiometers with multiturn structures. In them, we need to go further
than 360 degrees and the potentiometer allows for more rotations.
â€¢ Ranges for the translational system is from 2mm to 50cm while for the
rotational is 10 degrees to 50 rotations. Thus, it is very easy to implement.
The resistance range is very broad, from 10 ohm to 1M Ohm.
â€¢ Of course, we cannot use the value of the resistance in our data
acquisition, but we will need to have an input excitation voltage so that the
resistance can be translated into a voltage fell on the resistor. As such, it
works as a voltage divider. This can be easily seen from the translational
system. Thus, we need an excitation voltage. In most of the cases, a DC
voltage will be used, where we input DC voltage to obtain the divided DC
voltage. Sometimes, however, we can use AC. Why is this? Well, when
we have very small signals, we will need to amplify themand it is actually
easierto amplify an AC signal than a DC one. DC signals have a lot of
interference (like it could happen with the offset of an amplifier).
â€¢ How is the transfer function of this potentiometer? Actually, itis a linear
and proportional model, where the voltage grows proportional to the
motion or displacement.
â€¢ This is the inside of such a potentiometer. The wiper turns around and the
resistance takes certain value.
â€¢ What type of material do we need to have this change on the resistance.
â€¢ The most simpleand most used material is actually carbon
7
material. Carbon has the property of having a certain
resistance. It is not a very good conductor or a very good
insulator. It is something between the two. It is important to
know that carbon is not a semi conductor. A very good
property of carbon is that it is self lubricating which helps with
a very low friction coefficient. As we have a mechanical
component, there is some friction causing some erosion. So,
the lower the friction coefficient, the lower the erosion, the
longer the life time. Unfortunately, carbon does not have a
friction coefficient of 0, so it will wear out with time. It is also
very cheap. When do we use carbon? When we need
something that is not used very often (the wheel of a radio)
â€¢ Another material that can be used is a wire wound. It is a wire
which has a certain resistance. It is not a copper wire,
because copper has a very low resistance. It is mostly used
for power potentiometers. One very interesting property of
these potentiometers is the resolution. As the wiper moves
back and forth over the different resistance wires, the voltage
will be changing in steps. This is a difference as compared
with the carbon potentiometer, which are analogue, meaning
the resolution is 0.
â€¢ The best material in an application as a sensor which needs to
last long are the conducting plastics. It is a composite material
with extremely low friction coefficient. One of the lowest
friction coefficient is Tefflon. It is used in potentiometers. First,
the friction coefficient is so low that you do not need force to
turn it. If the friction is so low, the erosion is nearly non-
existentand the life-time is very veryhigh. However, it is
expensive.
â€¢ To sum up, the problems of potentiometers come in the form of
friction and resolution.

Now we can discuss the Gage factors of some materials. Constantan, which
is a material composed by niqueland cupper has a GF of 2.1, why shall we
use this material? Because the temperature coefficient is very verysmall. If
the temperature would change, also the resistor change. It could happen that
if the resistor change to apply is very small, the temperature has a bigger
temperature change than the one provoked by the strain and we need to
avoid this. Thus, we need to keep the temperature coefficient as low as
possible.
If we need materials that have a longer elongation range, we have other
materials such as the isoelastic. The GF is higher and the temperature coeff.
Is still ok.
One interesting case is the Nickel, which has a negative GF. How is this
possible? Remember that the GF is 1 + 2v + change in resistivity. 1 and 2v
are positive numbers, right? So, the factor comes from the change in
resistivity. This means that Nickel has a very strong negative piezo-electric
effect. Moreover, the temperature coefficient is very high.
We can also see the case of the semiconductor Sillicon, which has a very
high GF. This gives us the opposite behaviour. A very strong piezo-resistive
effect.
These are then piezo-resistive materials, because the strain is mainly
dependent on the piezo-resistivity. And we could create sensors with a
strong piezo-resistive effect or anti-piezo-resistive.
Unfortunaly, they also have very high temperature coefficient. This could
