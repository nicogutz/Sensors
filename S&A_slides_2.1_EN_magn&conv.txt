One concept that will come in handy when we talk about AC and induction motors:
transformers.
A transformer is two inductors magnetically coupled. It is often defined by its function.
In this situation, we have a ring with very high permeability (very low reluctance) so that
it easily lets flux be generated through and we have two sets of windings: a primary side
(e1) and a secondary side (e2).
Now suppose we apply a voltage at the primary side (constant; sinusoidal or squared),
as a result there will be a flux given by faradays law (a voltage causes a change in the
flux).
Together with the flux we will get a current im or magnetizing current (which is
proportional to the flux and in phase).
This flux is also going through the secondary side.
As Faraday goes in both directions, the changing flux will generate a voltage in the
second set of windings (e2).
Now, we can divide the two equations, we get the ratio of the voltage is equal to the ratio
of the number of turns (which is most probably a familiar equation).

Introduction to transformers: Eddy current losses
9
In the situation, we have the coil that goes around the ring. A changing flux will cause a
voltage in any close path (loop). Such at what we see in the first figure.
A voltage means a current flowing through the path. This means that the magnetic core
has a non-zero resistivity and it makes a loss.
To avoid this, we laminate the material of the magnetic core. You will see this in the lab
as well. So, we slice them, we put a bit of barnish (an insulator) and we glue them
together. In this way, the loop becomes smaller, the flux becomes tinier, making the
current very small, and so do the loss. So the eddy currents are reduced. In summary
we laminate to limit the eddy currents. The eddy currents are not always bad. For
instance, they can be used at the bottom of your pot for cooking with induction.

Electric machines (or motors) convert electrical power input into mechanical output.

We often describe electric machines by viewing a cross-section, as if the machine were
cut by a plane perpendicular to the shaft axis and viewed from one side.
Because of symmetry, this cross-section can be taken anywhere along the shaft axis.
In the second figure, we can see that all machines have a stationary part (called the
stator), a rotating part (call the rotor), separated by an air gap, thereby allowing the rotor
to rotate freely on a shaft (supported by bearings).
The stator is firmly affixed to a foundation to prevent it from turning.

13
In order to require small ampere-turns to create flux lines crossing the gap (figure a),
both the rotor and the stator are made up of high-permeability ferromagnetic materials,
and the length of the air gap is kept as small as possible.
The stator-produced flux distribution of figure a is shown for a 2-pole machine where the
field distribution corresponds to a combination of a single north pole and a single short
pole (figure a). This is the most common situation.
Often there are more than 2 poles, for example 4 or 6. This is the case of the figure b.
Due to complete symmetry around the periphery of the airgap, it is sufficient to consider
only one pole pair consisting of adjacent north and south poles. Other pole pairs have
identical conditions of magnetic fields and currents.
Finally, if the rotor and the stator are perfectly round, the air gap is uniform, and the
reluctance in the path of flux lines crossing the air gap is uniform. Machines with such
structures are called non-salient pole machines.
Sometimes, the machines are purposely designed to have saliency so that the magnetic
reluctance is unequal along various paths as shown in Figure c. This saliency is called
reluctance torque and it is a significant means of producing torque.
